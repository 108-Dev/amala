It was July 26, 1972.

Amsterdam.

A seventy-six year old man from Calcutta
walked into a temple room on the Herengracht
and sat down to install three Deities
before the cameras of Dutch television
and the reporters of the biggest newspapers in the city.

He asked for the fruits and grains.

They told him the fruits had been used
in the fruit salad.

He asked for the flowers.

They said they forgot.

He asked who was responsible.

Nobody answered.

He was not the least bit intimidated
by the cameras.
By the press.
By the full room of guests watching.

He was angry —
the way a father is angry
when something sacred
has been treated carelessly.

Then he looked at the altar.

The altar was too small.
The Deities could not fit.
The whole throne had to be dismantled
and carried to a corner of the hall.

He waited.

When they were finally placed —
dressed plainly,
the altar improvised,
nothing as it should have been —

he chanted the mantras.

All of them.

Then he delivered the full lecture.

Then Nandakumar, his servant,
went back for a plate of Jagannath's maha-prasadam.

It fell on the floor of the taxi
on the way back to the hotel.

Prabhupada ate it
without minding at all.

*Just imagine what level of consciousness that was.*

---

He placed those three Deities
in Amsterdam with his own hands
on a broken altar
in a fiasco of a ceremony
watched by cameras that would broadcast it to the city —

and he didn't flinch.
Not once.

Because Jagannath was there.

And where Jagannath is —
nothing else matters.

---

Those Deities were carved in 1971.
They were installed in 1972.
They were moved in 1981.

And Amsterdam has been borrowing them back
every summer since —

bringing them home for Ratha Yatra
then sending them away again.

---

This is a question.

Not an accusation.
Not a demand.

Just a question —

*Is it time to bring them home?*

---

*Jagannatha Svami*
*nayana-patha-gami bhavatu me*

O Lord of the Universe —
be the constant object of my vision.
