# Addendum: Dutch-Language Media and Archival Traces of Hare Krishna / ISKCON in Amsterdam (1970s–1990s)
## 1. Purpose of This Addendum
This addendum extends the earlier report on Srila Prabhupada, Amsterdam, and Jagannatha by incorporating Dutch-language historical, media, and archival sources that were not previously synthesised. It focuses on the spatial and temporal trajectory of ISKCON in Amsterdam, drawing primarily on local history articles, city-archive records, and Dutch contextual overviews.[^1][^2][^3][^4]

***
## 2. First Temple: Bethaniënstraat 39 (ca. 1970–1973)
### 2.1 Transformation from bicycle storage to Hare Krishna temple
Stadsherstel’s building history for Bethaniënstraat 37–39 notes that after a long sequence of owners and uses, including a broodbakkerij (bakery) and an anthracite fuel business (Maus), the underbuilt spaces at 39 and 41 were used for small workshops and a bicycle storage facility in the mid-20th century. In **1970**, this bicycle storage was “omgebouwd tot een heuse tempel” – converted into a genuine temple for the Hare Krishna movement, described as having arrived in Amsterdam at the end of the “verlichte” sixties.[^3]

The article emphasizes the symbolic continuity with the nearby Bethaniënklooster: “En zo liepen er 500 jaar na de vestiging van het Bethaniënklooster op deze plek weer monniken – sanyassins – door de Bethaniënstraat.” This explicitly describes the early ISKCON monks as a new generation of monastics inhabiting a historically religious street.[^3]
### 2.2 Community composition and daily life
Stadsherstel reports that around **1971** there were nine residents in the Bethaniënstraat temple: “Jan, een Frans meisje, een Nieuw-Zeelander en een Engelsman, twee Amerikanen en twee Nederlanders en de driejarige Thor.” The text notes that while the movement was generally peaceful, the constant early-morning chanting (beginning at sunrise) and the practice of leaving bowls of rice and vegetables on the sidewalk for travellers caused friction in the neighbourhood due to vermin.[^3]

The same source recounts that in **1972** “de geestelijk leider” visited Bethaniënstraat and that his followers, according to local lore, kissed the street where he had placed his sandals. The event is quoted from the book *Het Bethaniënblok in Amsterdam*, with the wry remark that “Zelden is de Bethaniënstraat zo schoon geweest.” Although Prabhupada is not named explicitly in this paragraph, the dating and description match other sources about Srila Prabhupada’s 1972 Amsterdam visit.[^3]
### 2.3 ONH confirmation and Prabhupada’s Amsterdam anecdotes
The regional history site ONH (Over de Noord-Hollandse geschiedenis) independently confirms that “de eerste Hare Krishna’s vestigen zich hier begin jaren ’70 in de Bethaniënstraat 39” and that they establish “de eerste Nederlandse Hare Krishna-tempel” at that address. ONH adds that the Hare Krishna devotees held Sunday gatherings at “de Kosmos,” a cultural centre on Prins Hendrikkade 142 (later Fantasio), and spent much time in the Vondelpark and in the city centre, singing and dancing among shoppers.[^1]

ONH explicitly names Srila Prabhupada and states that he visited Bethaniënstraat, led meetings, and walked every morning with a group of followers in the Vondelpark, where he passed sleeping hippies and famously commented: “Just see! In the material world, it does not matter whether one is rich or poor, there will always be some facility for sex life.” The article further records that he gave evening lectures in Hotel Krasnapolsky on Dam Square, attended by a diverse crowd of hippies and drunken troublemakers, and that a scuffle during one event led to a chastened troublemaker returning the next day to apologise and become a follower.[^1]

## 3. Subsequent Amsterdam Temple Locations and Timeline
### 3.1 Herengracht 96 (from 1973)
Both ONH and the Over Amsterdam article “Waar zijn de Hare Krishna’s van Amsterdam gebleven?” state that after Bethaniënstraat, a new temple was found in **1973** at **Herengracht 96**. The temple there maintained strict austerity, with devotees living simply while spending days chanting in procession through streets such as the Leidsestraat.[^5][^1]

Journalist and author Adriaan van Dis visited this Herengracht temple in **1975** and described the experience in NRC, noting vegetarian meals, temple dance, and a famously stark quote given to him: “Seks is als kauwgom. Na lang kauwen verliest het zijn smaak.” He also remarked on the presence of homeless people and drug users who came to the temple for free Sunday food, confirming the Hare Krishna temple’s role in social outreach as well as religious practice.[^1][^5]
### 3.2 Keizersgracht 94 (from 1980) and Ruysdaelkade 225
According to ONH and Over Amsterdam, the temple moved in **1980** to **Keizersgracht 94**, against a backdrop of internal and international ISKCON turmoil following Prabhupada’s death in 1977. Leadership crises and scandals reduced the number of Amsterdam devotees to around **30** by the end of the 1990s.[^5][^1]

The same sources state that from the Keizersgracht the community eventually moved to **Ruysdaelkade 225**, and that by that time the original temple deities (heiligdommen) had already been moved to a castle in Belgium, which other sources identify as Radhadesh. Back to Godhead and earlier ISKCON sources confirm that Jagannatha deities originally installed by Prabhupada in Amsterdam later resided in Radhadesh, forming a Benelux Jagannatha hub.[^1][^6][^5]

Back to Godhead’s 1987 and 1989 address listings support this: they list “Krsna Dham, 225 Ruysdaelkade, 1072 AW, Amsterdam” as the ISKCON centre, corroborating the transition highlighted in Dutch articles.[^7][^8]
### 3.3 Lizzy Ansinghstraat 80–1hs (current temple)
Abhay Charan’s Dutch-language overview, “Hare Krishna’s in Nederland,” notes that “in de tempel aan de Lizzy Ansinghstraat in Amsterdam kun je elke zondag gewoon terecht voor een vegetarische maaltijd,” confirming the current location as Lizzy Ansinghstraat. ONH likewise states that “Anno 2022 bevindt de tempel van de Hare Krishna’s zich in een bescheiden onderkomen aan de Lizzy Ansinghstraat 80–1hs,” managed by a small number of devotees and still offering Sunday meals and an annual festival on Dam Square where around a thousand vegetarian meals are distributed.[^1][^2]

These Dutch sources align precisely with the English-language ISKCON Amsterdam website, which lists Lizzy Ansinghstraat 80 as the temple address and describes Ratha Yatra parades from De Pijp to Dam Square.[^9]

## 4. City Archive Photographs and Visual Traces
### 4.1 Stadsarchief Amsterdam image bank
The Stadsarchief Amsterdam (city archive) holds several photographic records indexing Hare Krishna activity in the city. For example, entries titled “Hare Krishna beweging, Van Hilligaertstraat 17” show interior views of an ISKCON temple space in Amsterdam. These images depict a temple room with an altar, religious paraphernalia, and devotees, and their catalog metadata ties them to the later phase when the community was located away from the canal belt.[^4][^10]

The Stadsherstel article on Bethaniënstraat 37–39 lists Stadsarchief Amsterdam, Delpher, and the local history book *Het Bethaniënblok in Amsterdam* as key sources for its narrative, indicating that the city archive contains additional, not yet fully explored, photographic and textual materials on early Hare Krishna presence in Bethaniënstraat.[^3]
### 4.2 Other archival leads
Finding aids such as the Dolf Toussaint archive (Stadsarchief access number 30997) and the Bert Nienhuis archive (30681) mention photographs of Hare Krishna on the Dam, Nieuwendijk, and elsewhere in Amsterdam, as well as “voedseluitdeling bij de Hare Krishna, mogelijk in de Bethaniënstraat.” These descriptions suggest that detailed visual documentation of street chanting and food distribution exists, though accessing the specific photographs requires further work with the archive’s finding aids and high-resolution scans.[^11][^12]

The Rob Zwetsloot and Rob Mieremet collections include images labelled “Hare Krishna aanhanger spreekt voorbijganger aan” in Amsterdam, possibly near Dam Square or during events at Hotel Krasnapolsky. These offer additional visual testimony to the movement’s visibility in the city centre during the 1970s.[^13][^14]

## 5. Dutch Contextual Narratives and Media Memory
### 5.1 National overviews
Abhay Charan’s article, “Hare Krishna’s in Nederland,” frames the arrival of the movement as an accident of immigration policy: a group of Canadian devotees, rejected at UK customs, travelled on to Amsterdam and founded a temple where Prabhupada’s teachings were propagated. It confirms that Srila Prabhupada visited the Dutch temple in **1972** and gave a talk at Hotel Krasnapolsky, and it sketches the community’s ascetic lifestyle (no alcohol, tobacco, drugs, meat, coffee, or tea; early rising; cold showers; long mantra chanting sessions).[^2]

The article further notes that after Prabhupada’s death, several successors failed their responsibilities, causing a decline in the Dutch movement and the arrest of two ex-Hare Krishna members for drug smuggling in **1979**, triggering a police investigation. This context matches the ONH account of a shrinking Amsterdam Hare Krishna population and multiple relocations of the temple through the 1980s and 1990s.[^1][^2]
### 5.2 Amsterdam-focused retrospectives
The Over Amsterdam/ONH piece “Waar zijn de Hare Krishna’s van Amsterdam gebleven?” (which shares content with the ONH article) presents Hare Krishna as a quintessential element of 1970s Amsterdam’s countercultural street life. The article emphasises:[^5][^1]

- Early chants and public processions in Bethaniënstraat, Vondelpark, and inner-city shopping streets.
- Strict ascetic practices and the visual distinctiveness of devotees (shaved heads, sikhas, tilaka, saffron robes).[^1][^5]
- A sequence of temple addresses: Bethaniënstraat 39, Herengracht 96 (from 1973), Keizersgracht 94 (from 1980), Ruysdaelkade 225, and finally Lizzy Ansinghstraat 80–1hs.[^5][^1]
- The relocation of temple deities to a Belgian castle, consistent with the Radhadesh Jagannatha connection.[^6][^1][^5]

The same retrospective underscores that despite scandals and decline, a small Hare Krishna presence persists in Amsterdam, with a yearly festival on Dam Square distributing large numbers of free vegetarian meals—an event that dovetails with ISKCON Amsterdam’s publicised Ratha Yatra and Chariot Parade festivals.[^9][^1]

***
## 6. Relationship to Prabhupada and Jagannatha Narrative
### 6.1 Confirming Prabhupada’s Amsterdam visit from Dutch sources
Dutch-language articles (ONH and Abhay Charan) independently confirm that Srila Prabhupada visited Amsterdam in **1972**, visited the Bethaniënstraat temple, walked in the Vondelpark with disciples, and gave lectures in Hotel Krasnapolsky. These details corroborate the English-language recollections and SrilaPrabhupadaLila narratives used in the main report, adding local colour and specific commentary (e.g., Prabhupada’s remark about sex life in the park, and the altercation with drunk troublemakers at Krasnapolsky).[^1][^15][^16][^17][^2]

Although the Dutch sources do not explicitly mention the Jagannatha installation or deity names, their confirmation of Prabhupada’s presence in 1972, at Bethaniënstraat and Krasnapolsky, lines up precisely with ISKCON narratives that place the Jagannatha installation in the same city and time frame.[^15][^6][^1]
### 6.2 Connecting deities’ relocation to Radhadesh
ONH and Over Amsterdam mention that “de tempelheiligdommen” were moved from Amsterdam to a Belgian castle as the community contracted. Back to Godhead’s survey of global Rathayatra traditions specifies that Prabhupada-installed Jagannatha deities from Amsterdam later resided in Radhadesh and travelled for festivals in Benelux cities, including Amsterdam.[^5][^1][^6]

Taken together, these sources tie the Dutch-language reference to “heiligdommen” and the “kasteel in België” to the specific Jagannatha deities discussed in the main report, strengthening the cross-lingual link between Amsterdam’s early temple and its later role as a node in the Radhadesh Jagannatha network.[^1][^6]

***
## 7. Next Archival Directions Based on New Leads
The Dutch materials reviewed here also sharpen potential next steps for still deeper research:

- **Bethaniënstraat-focused archival work:** Because Stadsherstel cites *Het Bethaniënblok in Amsterdam* and Delpher as sources, a more targeted search of Delpher for 1970–1973 using combinations like “Hare Krishna Bethaniënstraat,” “Hare Krishna klooster,” and “Kosmos Prins Hendrikkade” could reveal contemporaneous local reporting.[^1][^3]
- **City-archive photo series:** Finding aids for Dolf Toussaint, Bert Nienhuis, and Rob Zwetsloot/Zwetsloot suggest series of photographs of Hare Krishna processions and food distribution at specific Amsterdam locations (Dam, Nieuwendijk, Bethaniënstraat), which could be systematically identified and catalogued.[^11][^13][^12]
- **Beeld & Geluid TV footage:** The existence of Dutch retrospectives and the high visibility of Hare Krishna in the 1970s make it likely that Beeld & Geluid holds additional non-digitised footage; the “Het archief” portal suggests that some materials are accessible only on-site, implying that a dedicated research trip could be valuable.[^18][^19][^20]

These leads, combined with the English and Dutch textual sources already synthesised, provide a more firmly triangulated picture of Hare Krishna / ISKCON’s spatial history in Amsterdam and its connection to Srila Prabhupada and Jagannatha worship.
